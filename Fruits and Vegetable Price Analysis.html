Fruits and Vegetable Price Study

Introduction

Considering the composition of foods within an average meal, how much does price constitute your decisions when it comes to choosing what youâ€™ll next be creating a meal with, or snacking on throughout the day? The FDA has conducted research on this particular subject, comparing the prices of healthy and unhealthy (less healthy) foodstuffs 1; considering whether price is a barrier towards healthier eating.2

In creating this research, and aiming to understand the average price of produce, the FDA has so far managed four years of average price per units for fruits and vegetables 3, alongside Cup Equivalent Price (CEP, or the price of a cup made of the edible weight of a produce item). This appeared to be a fun set of data to analyze, so I have performed a brief overview on what is being said about the 2022 year (most recent) report on Fruit and Vegetable Prices.

Analyses

Preliminary Preparation

All year files provided by the FDA on the post have been extracted and joined into a large data frame, for the sake of practice and condensation (involving the conversion of excel sheets into digestible tibbles, as well as condensing the labels of diverse fruits and veggies to access readily through dplyr). New columns for identification of year and distinguishing forms and types were created; below is the entirety of the data if such would be the desire to quickly survey it.

suppressWarnings(library(reactable))

Distribution of Cup Equivalent Price

Firstly, comparing fruit and vegetables CEP distribution, to get an idea of how much it costs for a cup of edible within these two categories of edible plants, and against each other. Below is a histogram for both.

everything_all |>
  filter(Form == "Fresh" & Year == 2022) |>
  ggplot(aes(CupEquivalentPrice, fill = Category)) +
  geom_histogram(binwidth = 0.25, alpha = 0.75) +
  facet_grid(cols = vars(Category)) +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#B04A41", "#41B071", "#332827")) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(0, 3, by = .5)) +
  labs(
    fill = "Category",
    x = "Cup Equivalent Price ($USD)",
    y = "Count",
    title = "Edible Cup Price for Fresh Fruits and Vegetables in 2022",
    subtitle = "Vegetables appear to have a slightly lower average Edible Cup Price than Fruits."
  )

Clearly, there are more vegetables than fruits to account for (at the very least those under the designation of â€œFreshâ€, as opposed to â€œcannedâ€, â€œdriedâ€, or â€œjuiceâ€). Still, we can see the vast majority of our vegetables are in the bucket which hovers around $0.50 per cup equivalent price. Conversely, most of our fresh fruits are around $0.75 or $1.00.

Distribution of Yields for Fruits and Vegetables

Interesting and implicit within this collection of data, is the governmentâ€™s department consideration for how much of a fruit or vegetable remains if you consider only the edible weight, represented within the frames as the â€œYieldâ€ (or the ratio of weight of a produce item after the inedible parts are removed to the total weight). What is being used here is Handbook 102 of the U.S. Department of Agriculture.4 In the figure below, we can see the distribution of the yields for fruits and vegetables.

everything_all |>
  filter(Form == "Fresh" & Year == 2022) |>
  ggplot(aes(Yield, fill = Category)) +
  geom_histogram(binwidth = 0.1, alpha = 0.75) +
  facet_grid(cols = vars(Category)) +
  scale_fill_manual(values = c("#B04A41", "#41B071", "#332827")) +
  scale_y_continuous(breaks = seq(0,13, by = 2)) +
  scale_x_continuous(breaks = seq(0,100, by = .2)) +
  labs(
    fill = "Category",
    x = "Yield (%)",
    y = "Count",
    title = "Yield of Fruits and Vegetables (%)",
    subtitle = "According to FDA"
  )

A good deal of fruits are around ~90% edible, though a good number of commonly purchased fruits appear to yield less edible weight than this (almost half of them). Vegetables are more numerous around the 80% mark, and interestingly, yield even further than 100% for some; upon doing research and contacting the people maintaining the dataset, this is under the consideration that some vegetables (in this case kale, collard greens, and brussels sprouts), gain weight when cooked or are processed in such a way that they can be consumed.
